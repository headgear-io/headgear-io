Beyond the Camera Frame

See the world around you

360 cameras range the gamut from consumer to professional. With so many formats and resolutions it's hard to know what to use, but actually the most important part is why the narrative should be told in 360.

Immersive Storytelling is wildly different when it comes to Cinematic VR with a lot of care taken about the 3D audio experience, cut scene changes and camera movement (to avoid motion sickness) using radio controlled grips.

The boundaries between audience and action are slowly slipping away, because there is no behind the camera anymore. The viewer is completely immersed in the environment and as storytellers new techniques are used to direct one's attention.

All the best practices from storyboarding to post-production has changed. Engaging the viewer in completely new ways, whether they're using the browser, mobile or headset has to be taken into account.

Go with the moment

Overlay a live filmed person and their actions in Virtual Reality and record the 3D modelled scene as the background. This is the easiest way to show someone VR without actually putting them in it.

This in effect is a marketing tool to explain and show-off these technologies in traditional video formats. This way any spectator can view the scene.

Using a green screen and camera tracking technology it is possible to place anyone into a Virtual Environment. Interestingly, spectator VR can be achieved the same way with built in streaming functionality for e-sports.

Another methods for introducing live streaming of people into VR includes sensor depth-mapping. This actually requires even less production time to set up.

The Future is...

AUGMENTED

The trends and forecasts reveal that the future to be mobile augmented. We already contextualising the real world with data and Augmented Reality and Artificial Intelligence will play big role in making sense of that informaiton.

It is a combination of computer vision, SLAM (simultaneous localisation and mapping), and natural language processing that will allow us to understand our environment more readily.

The first wave of mobile augmented reality (using camera phones to overlay 3D models, holograms and data on your handheld screen) is already available on over 400 million smartphone devices, and is projected to hit 2 billion devices by the end of 2018.

The use cases and examples are amassing, just check out this YouTube playlist for more on the fast growing options in this field.

The next waves of AR will be focused on smartglasses that are either tethered or untethered. There are hurdles here to overcome, but are getting tackled on all ends. These include the low-cost production of optics, the issue of power for device efficiency and battery supply tech, and finally of course the glasses' form factor and its user interface.

When it comes to the User Experience in Augmented Reality one of the biggest unknowns is how users will engage with. The Human Computer Interaction is moving beyond simple touch to a more gestured and voice-based sequence.

CAD & Point Clouds

Mapping the World

Digitising the 3D world is part and parcel of delivering on the promise of these technologies, which means being able to scan and/or replicate the real world.

It also means that this digtal data can be appropriated not only for virtual and augmented realities, but also delivered over the internet for browser viewing and catalogued there too.

The trick here is how to optimise this data and visuals for VR/AR. This means building the right pipeline from model/scan to consumption and analysis.
